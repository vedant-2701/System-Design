# Question

> You're running a high-throughput Go service making 800 short-lived HTTPS connections per second to the same upstream. You've correctly sized MaxIdleConnsPerHost to 200.
Given what you now know about the 4-way handshake — which side enters TIME_WAIT, and why does it matter which side it is for your service's port exhaustion problem?

---

# Answer

## Who Closes the Connection?

When you size MaxIdleConnsPerHost to 200, but your throughput is 800 requests/second, 200 connections will remain safely in your pool and be reused. The remaining 600 concurrent connections must be closed.
- If your Go service closes them: Your server initiates the 4-way handshake (FIN packet). Your local operating system and NAT gateway enter TIME_WAIT.
- If the upstream API closes them: The upstream server sends the FIN packet first. The upstream server enters TIME_WAIT.

**"If upstream initiates, that's their concern"** — this thinking will cause you real production incidents.

Here's why it matters to you even when the upstream closes:

If the upstream server sends FIN first, it enters TIME_WAIT — true, their port problem. But what happens to your side? Your Go service receives FIN, sends ACK, and the connection is fully closed on your end. That connection slot in your pool is now freed.

**But here's the real problem:** if the upstream closes connections aggressively — say after every request, or after a short keep-alive timeout — your pool never actually stays full. Connections get closed under you before you can reuse them. You're back to creating new connections constantly, paying handshake cost on every request, even with a correctly sized pool.

This is a real operational issue called **keep-alive mismatch.**

```
Your pool thinks connection is idle and reusable
Upstream has already closed it (sent FIN)
Your service tries to reuse it → gets RST or broken pipe
→ request fails, connection discarded, new one created
```

Go's `http.Transport` handles this somewhat — it detects dead connections and retries. But it adds latency and wastes pool slots.

**So you always need to know both sides' keep-alive settings.** Tune your `IdleConnTimeout` to be slightly less than the upstream's keep-alive timeout so you close connections before the upstream does — making your side initiate the close, keeping TIME_WAIT on your side where you can reason about it, and avoiding broken pipe errors.

## Complete TCP Lifecycle — Unified View

Let me give you the full picture in one place now:

```
CLOSED
  ↓ (client sends SYN)
SYN_SENT
  ↓ (server sends SYN-ACK, client sends ACK)
ESTABLISHED ←————————————————————————————————
  ↓                                          |
  ↓ data flows                               |
  ↓                                          |
[whoever sends FIN first]                    |
  ↓                                          |
FIN_WAIT_1 (sent FIN)                        |
  ↓ (receives ACK)                           |
FIN_WAIT_2 (waiting for other side's FIN)    |
  ↓ (receives FIN, sends ACK)                |
TIME_WAIT (2*MSL = 60sec) ———————————————————
  ↓
CLOSED
```

The other side:
```
ESTABLISHED
  ↓ (receives FIN, sends ACK)
CLOSE_WAIT  ← your service is here if upstream closes first
  ↓ (application closes, sends FIN)
LAST_ACK
  ↓ (receives ACK)
CLOSED
```

**CLOSE_WAIT is a critical state to know.** If your application receives FIN but never calls `close()` on the socket — maybe a bug, goroutine leak, or you're not reading the response body fully in Go — the connection stays in CLOSE_WAIT forever. You accumulate CLOSE_WAIT connections, exhaust file descriptors, and the service dies.

In your Linux fundamentals session you diagnosed CLOSE_WAIT with `ss -tan`. This is exactly the scenario that produces it.
